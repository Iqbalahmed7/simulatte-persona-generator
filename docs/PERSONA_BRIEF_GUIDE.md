# Persona Brief Guide

**Owner:** Iqbal · **Last updated:** 2026-04-26

---

## How to write a brief

Specificity beats length. A four-sentence brief with concrete details produces a better persona than a five-paragraph one full of generic adjectives.

---

## The four ingredients

A brief that lands above 8.0 on the Genuineness score has four things:

1. **Demographic anchor** — name, age, city, household. The persona's structural frame.
2. **Economic context** — income bracket or occupation seniority. Determines what they can afford and how they think about money.
3. **One distinctive habit or contradiction** — what they actually do, not what they say. Subscribes to five streaming services. Eats out four times a week despite saying they want to cook more. Reads MKBHD reviews before every gadget purchase.
4. **A signal of values or constraints** — what matters to them, or what they refuse to compromise on. Rents because they don't want to commit. Brand-loyal to local Indian companies. Will only buy from sellers with verified reviews.

You don't need all four. Three out of four lands at 8/10. Two lands at 6/10. One lands at 4/10 and reads like a stock photo.

---

## Three examples that work

### Ravi

> Ravi, 34, software engineer in Bangalore. Single, rents, ₹18L/year. Reads MKBHD reviews, follows Reddit r/IndiaInvestments, eats out four times a week despite "wanting to cook more."

What this brief does well: demographic anchor (name, age, city, income), economic context (₹18L, rents), a distinctive habit (MKBHD reviews + Reddit investing), and a contradiction (eats out vs. aspiration to cook). Four out of four. Genuineness score typically lands 8.5–9.2.

---

### Margaret

> Margaret, 67, retired school principal in rural Devon. Lives alone in a stone cottage, fixed pension, grows her own vegetables. Brand-loyal to Marks & Spencer, distrusts anything bought online. Her granddaughter set her up with WhatsApp last year and she now uses it daily but refuses to message strangers.

What this brief does well: strong demographic anchor with life-stage context, economic constraint (fixed pension), a vivid contradiction (uses WhatsApp daily but won't message strangers), and a clear values signal (brand loyalty, digital distrust). The WhatsApp detail is load-bearing — it tells the system exactly where to anchor her technology comfort floor.

---

### Priya

> Priya, 28, marketing manager at a Mumbai DTC beauty startup. Earns ₹14L plus equity. Lives with two flatmates in Bandra, sends ₹20K home to her parents in Pune monthly. Spends impulsively on skincare and books, frugal on everything else. Suspicious of any brand with a celebrity face.

What this brief does well: precise economic context (income + remittance = real budget constraint), a clear spending contradiction (impulsive in two categories, frugal elsewhere), and a values signal that shapes her brand response directly. The celebrity-face aversion is a concrete decision rule — the persona will fire it in probe responses without being told to.

## What to skip

- **Vague adjectives** — "tech-savvy," "health-conscious," "value-driven." Every persona is tech-savvy relative to someone. Tell us what they actually use.
- **Personality-test labels** — "introvert," "INTJ," "Type A." These collapse behaviour into categories. Describe the behaviour instead.
- **Aspirational gloss** — "loves life, family, and good food." This describes no one and everyone.
- **Real public figures** — flagged automatically and rejected.

## Scoring reference

| Ingredients present | Genuineness floor | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 4 / 4 | 8.5 | Strong anchor, economic context, behaviour, values all present |
| 3 / 4 | 7.5–8.5 | Missing one — usually values or economic context |
| 2 / 4 | 6.0–7.5 | Functional but thin — persona answers questions but lacks texture |
| 1 / 4 | 4.0–6.0 | Stock-photo level — correct type, no individual |
